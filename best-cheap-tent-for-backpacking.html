Backpacking tents play by different rules than car-camping tents. Every ounce you carry on your back matters. Packed size has to fit inside or alongside your pack. And durability needs to survive rocky ground, trail abuse, and weather that you can't just drive away from.

The problem? Good ultralight tents are expensive. The Big Agnes Copper Spur, MSR Hubba Hubba, and Nemo Hornet all cost $350 or more. If you're just getting into backpacking — or you go a few times a year and can't justify that price — there's one tent that dominates the budget ultralight conversation.

The Naturehike Cloud-Up 2 is the tent that r/ultralight, backpacking YouTube, and budget hiking forums all agree on. At 3.6 pounds with aluminum poles, a 20D silnylon fly, and double-wall construction, it reads like a tent that should cost $250+. It costs under $100.

The double-wall design is the key differentiator from other budget options. Single-wall tents (common at this price) trap condensation inside — you wake up with water dripping on your sleeping bag. The Cloud-Up's separate inner mesh body and rainfly create an air gap that dramatically reduces condensation. It's not perfect in humid conditions, but it's worlds better than the single-wall alternatives.

The aluminum poles are another standout. Most budget tents use fiberglass, which is heavier and snaps under pressure. Aluminum flexes and springs back — it's what premium tents use. The Cloud-Up also includes a vestibule for gear storage and boot protection, and it's freestanding, meaning it works on rocky ground where staking isn't possible.

Where it falls short: "2-person" is generous. Two average-sized adults will be shoulder to shoulder. It's ideal as a roomy 1-person tent or a tight 2-person for couples who don't mind close quarters. The included stakes are thin aluminum that bend on rocky ground. And the zippers can stick in cold weather.

If you're doing short, easy backpacking trips (1–2 miles to a backcountry campsite) and don't want to invest in a dedicated backpacking tent yet, the Sundome can work in a pinch. What to Look for in a Budget Backpacking Tent

Backpacking tents have different priorities than car-camping tents. Here's what matters most, in order:

Weight. The single most important spec. Under 4 lbs for a 2-person tent is the budget ultralight sweet spot. Under 3 lbs gets into serious ultralight territory (and serious prices). Over 5 lbs and you'll feel every ounce after the first mile.

Packed size. Your tent needs to fit inside your pack or strap to the outside without being unwieldy. The Cloud-Up at 16" × 5" slides into most packs easily. Bulkier tents require external lashing and throw off your pack balance.

Pole material. Aluminum poles are lighter, stronger, and more resilient than fiberglass. At the budget level, fiberglass is standard — aluminum is a major bonus. The Cloud-Up's aluminum poles are a big part of what makes it exceptional at this price.

Wall design. Double-wall tents (separate inner body + rainfly) manage condensation far better than single-wall tents. You'll pay slightly more for double-wall, but waking up to a dry interior is worth it.

Vestibule. A vestibule is the covered area outside the tent door where you store boots and gear. Without one, everything goes inside the tent (using up sleeping space) or gets left in the rain. The Cloud-Up's vestibule is modest but functional.

Budget vs. Premium Ultralight — Is It Worth the Upgrade?

The honest answer: it depends on how often you backpack.

The Naturehike Cloud-Up 2 at ~$90 gives you about 80% of the performance of a $350 Big Agnes Copper Spur UL2. The premium tent is lighter (2.5 lbs vs. 3.6 lbs), has better interior volume, higher-quality zippers, and more refined details. But the core function — a lightweight double-wall shelter with aluminum poles — is the same.

If you backpack 2–5 times a year, the Cloud-Up is the smart buy. Save the $260 difference for other gear. If you backpack 10+ times a year or do multi-day thru-hikes, the premium tent's weight savings and durability will pay for themselves over time.

Our take: Start with the Cloud-Up. If you fall in love with backpacking and wear it out in 2–3 seasons, upgrade to a premium tent knowing exactly what features matter most to you. Starting cheap lets you learn your preferences without a $350 mistake.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 3.6 lbs really considered ultralight?

It's on the heavier end of ultralight. True UL shelters run 1.5–2.5 lbs, but those cost $300+. At under $100, 3.6 lbs is exceptional. For context, most budget tents in this price range weigh 5–8 lbs. The Cloud-Up is roughly half the weight of a Coleman Sundome.

Can the Cloud-Up 2 really fit 2 people?

Technically yes, but it's snug. Two average-sized adults (under 5'10") can sleep side by side. Two larger adults will be very close. We recommend it as a roomy 1-person tent or a cozy 2-person for couples. If you need real 2-person space on a budget, consider the Cloud-Up 3 (slightly larger).

How does the Cloud-Up handle rain?

Well, but not perfectly. The 20D silnylon fly with taped seams handles steady rain. The bathtub floor keeps ground moisture out. Where it struggles: prolonged heavy downpours can eventually seep through, and if the fly sags onto the inner mesh, water wicks through contact points. Guy it out taut and you'll be fine in most conditions.

What's the best sleeping pad for a budget backpacking setup?

A closed-cell foam pad (Thermarest Z-Lite, ~$30–$45) is the budget standard — lightweight, indestructible, and doubles as a sit pad. If you want more comfort, inflatable pads like the Klymit Static V (~$45–$60) pack smaller but risk punctures. Either way, never skip the pad — the ground steals body heat fast.

Naturehike vs. Lanshan vs. 3F UL Gear — which budget UL tent is best?

The Cloud-Up 2 wins for most people because it's freestanding (doesn't require trekking poles) and double-wall. The Lanshan tents are lighter but require trekking poles for setup and are single-wall (more condensation). 3F UL Gear is similar to Naturehike in quality. If you use trekking poles, the Lanshan 2 is worth a look. Otherwise, the Cloud-Up is the safer, more versatile choice.
